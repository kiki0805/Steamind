How to use Steamind

Welcome to Steamind! To visualize your own Steam library, please fill in your SteamID in the input field at the top left corner. If you don't have a Steam account or just want to explore the visualization, there are some prefetched SteamIDs you can take a look at.

At the top right there is a legend. You can filter on game categories by clicking on their corresponding circle in the legend, or by using the filters below the visualization. Above the filters there is a current filters which shows you all the filters you have on right now. You can delete a filter by clicking on it

To reset the visualization and reset all the current filters, you can click on the reset button available at the right side of the filters bar.

If you wish to take a closer look at some games, you can click on them to show their info below the Game Info. By clicking on them you will lock them in place for easier organization. Click on them again to unlock their positions.

Those are the basics. Happy exploring!

About

Steamind is a tool for visualizing games from your own Steam library as well as other games from Steam that might suit your interests. It is the final project for the course DH2321 Information Visualization given at KTH Royal Institute of Technology. Initially inpsired by Steam's own recommendation system, we wanted to explore a different way of visualizing the data. By displaying the games that you own, Steamind aims to provide a way for you to gain insight of your own gaming preferences such as which categories you prefer, or help you come to terms with the uncomfortable thruth about your bundle-spending habits. Apart from that, Steamind also visualizes a selected set of other games that you might not own in hopes that you can use the visualizaiton to expand your library with games you might want to play.

The visualization consists of a force-directed graph organized in a hierarchical structure with two levels. Starting at the root node is the user and four branches representing game categories emerges from it. At the last level, we have the leaves which represent the games. The games are organized into these four main categories based on a research article from 2020 by Xiaozhou Li and Boyang Zhang [1].

The Data

The data was retrieved from various sources such as the Steam Web API, and Club 250. Data was also scraped using Scrapy from the Steam store itself. Ultimately, the database includes around 4000 game entries over 10000 games sold in Steam, and covers the most-played games while the other unrecorded ones are less-played. For each game, nominal, numerical, and categorical data was retrieved. These include names, header images, review ratios, (user's) playtime, popularity, category, tags, etc. All the information are organized into mainly six database models: Game, User, Tag, Genre, Review, Playtime. (More details)

Since the visualization is dynamic and changes depending on which user ID is currently being used, the data itself also needs to change dynamically. To achieve this, a custom API was created so that the front-end and back-end could communicate more efficiently. This means that the data model consists of a back-end database stored in SQLite and data retrieved in .json-format through the front-end.

Learning Objectives

Working with this project, we have gained a better understanding of the theory of information visualization as well as how it can be put into practice. Following the visualization pipeline, we have learnt how to gather and process data in order to get a workable structure. We have gained experience in the designing process and also gained a deeper knowledge in how to use the D3.js library to implement the visual structures and visual mappings needed for our vision to come true. Furthermore, we had to learn how to combine back-end and front-end to make it all work. This not only improved our overall programming skills, but also our teamwork and time management skills as we had to communicate and collaborate efficiently while taking other courses.
